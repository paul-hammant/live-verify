LIVE VERIFY

Verify printed claims from the issuer’s domain.

Live Verify: the next logical step up from the iPhone's Live Text.

There’s always something on paper (or on screen) that looks official, but may not be authentic. Or it could be out of date or revoked. "Live Verify" is an enhanced camera app that goes one step beyond iOS "Live Text" and Android's "Lens" text feature to become trust engine: “does the issuer currently attest to the validity of this exact text?”

Why this matters

  • A trust primitive for the physical world: readable text becomes machine-checkable authenticity, without replacing it with QR codes or forcing closed apps.
  • Sovereign, no gatekeeper: issuers publish from their own domain; verifiers decide who to trust; there’s no single global registry to control access.
  • Privacy-forward by default: OCR + hashing stay on-device; the network step is just a hash lookup.
  • Anti-fraud without friction: reduces forged IDs, credentials, and permits at the point of use (hotels, employers, venues) with a camera UX people already know.
  • Accessible and inclusive: works with paper, screenshots, and low-end printers; no NFC chips, special ink, or new hardware—just the camera stack most phones already have (screen moiré patterns may require higher-capability OCR).
  • Resilient: hashes can be published cheaply (even static hosting) while sensitive source records remain protected; verification can keep working during partial outages.
  • Commercial: issuer-side registry/integrations and verifier-side managed app/SDK are both natural SaaS offerings.

What the user sees

The camera app keeps the flow simple: “Snap” to capture the document, then it either overlays “Claim Verified” (or synonym) with issuer/domain shown, or Denied/Revoked, or "Not found" if the hash check fails to match a URL with a 200 response.

  • Snap—frame the registration marks, tap to capture.
  • Verified or Denied—an overlay tells you the outcome instantly.

Implementation

Snap → OCR → normalize (rules) → SHA-256 → GET issuer URL → Claim verified or denied.

Documents carry a verify: line pointing at the issuer’s domain, e.g. verify:issuer.example.com/claims
which implies a lookup like
https://issuer.example.com/claims/<sha256>.
You decide upfront whether issuer.example.com is an authority on the claim, then trust the camera app to execute the hash check and surface “Claim Verified” accordingly.

1
Snap
Capture the framed document.
2
OCR + normalize
Extract and normalize text locally.
3
SHA-256
Hash the normalized text.
4
Verify
GET the issuer URL; 404 = not found, 200 = status returned (OK / REVOKED / etc.).

What “Verified” means (and doesn’t)

  • Verified means: the domain in the verify: line currently attests to the hash for the extracted text.
  • It is not “ground truth”: the issuer can be wrong, malicious, or out-of-date.
  • The human decides whether that domain is an authority for the claim in question.
  • Look out for lookalike/typosquatted domains: while much of verification is automated the domain should be shown prominently for humans to judge or not as part of a "trust or don't trust" decision.

Verifications are revocable

Revocation is part of trust. Issuers can update or withdraw claims over time, so the result reflects what they stand behind today. Some issuers may return an operational instruction, e.g. Stolen ID — please retain/cut in half and contact https://example.comp/stolen_and_found quoting reference 283762..

Privacy model

OCR + normalization + SHA-256 happen on-device, and the only network step is a hash lookup. The science of one-way hashes is explained at one-way-hash.html, and we go into the rest of the guarantees in the Privacy Declaration.
