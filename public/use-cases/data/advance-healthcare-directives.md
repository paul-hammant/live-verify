## Data Verified

Patient/principal name, date of birth, healthcare agent (proxy) name and contact information, alternate agents, specific medical treatment preferences, life-sustaining treatment wishes, organ donation preferences, witness names and signatures, notary name and commission number (where required), directive date, any revocations or amendments.

**Document Types:**
- **Living Will:** Written instructions for end-of-life care
- **Healthcare Power of Attorney:** Designates agent for medical decisions
- **Combined Directive:** Living will and healthcare POA together
- **Do Not Resuscitate (DNR) Orders:** Physician orders for resuscitation decisions
- **POLST/MOLST Forms:** Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment
- **Mental Health Directive:** Preferences for psychiatric treatment
- **Anatomical Gift Declaration:** Organ and tissue donation wishes

**Life-or-Death Verification:** These documents directly determine medical treatment in critical situations. Hospital verification before implementing life-sustaining treatment decisions is essential.

## Data Visible After Verification

Shows the issuer domain (registry, notary, or healthcare system) and the responder text.

**Status Indications:**
- **Valid** - Directive is current and in effect
- **Revoked** - Principal has revoked the directive
- **Superseded** - Replaced by newer directive
- **Expired** - Directive has time limitation that has passed

**Agent Confirmation:** Verification may confirm designated agent: "Valid - Healthcare agent: [name]."

## Verification Architecture

**The Advance Directive Fraud Problem**

Fraudulent directives can harm patients:

- **Forged Directives:** Fake documents overriding patient's true wishes
- **Altered Preferences:** Genuine directives with modified treatment choices
- **Unauthorized Agents:** False healthcare proxy designations
- **Revocation Fraud:** False claims that directive was revoked
- **Outdated Directives:** Superseded documents presented as current
- **Capacity Fraud:** Directives executed by incapacitated persons

OCR-to-hash addresses forgery and alteration. Registry verification confirms current status.

**Registries and Healthcare Systems as Issuers**

Multiple parties may verify directives:

**State Registries:** Official state advance directive registries.

**Healthcare Systems:** Hospital and health system databases.

**Attorney Records:** Law firms maintaining client directives.

**National Registries:** US Living Will Registry and similar services.

**Notary Records:** Notarized directive verification through notary.

Each maintains verification endpoints for directives in their custody.

**Witnessing and Notarization Requirements**

Execution requirements vary:

**Witness Requirements:** Most states require two witnesses.

**Notarization:** Some states require or recommend notarization.

**Who Cannot Witness:** Restrictions on who can serve as witness.

**Healthcare Provider Witnesses:** Special rules for provider witnesses.

Verification confirms proper execution: "Verified - Two witnesses and notarized [date]."

**State Law Variations**

Laws differ significantly:

**Form Requirements:** State-specific form requirements.

**Agent Authority:** What decisions agents can make.

**Default Surrogates:** Who decides without designated agent.

**Reciprocity:** Recognition of out-of-state directives.

Verification indicates governing law: "Verified under [state] law."

**POLST/MOLST Protocols**

Physician orders for immediate effect:

**Physician Signature:** Requires physician signature, unlike living will.

**Portable Orders:** Travel with patient between care settings.

**Scope:** Addresses specific treatments (CPR, intubation, feeding tubes).

**Regular Review:** Should be reviewed periodically.

**Color-Coded Forms:** Many states use bright-colored forms for visibility.

POLST verification is particularly time-sensitive given emergency use.

**Mental Health Directives**

Psychiatric advance directives:

**Treatment Preferences:** Preferred medications, ECT, hospitalization.

**Inpatient vs. Outpatient:** Preferences for care settings.

**Psychotropic Medications:** Specific medication preferences.

**Override Provisions:** When directive can be overridden.

Mental health directives may have different verification requirements.

**Revocation and Amendment**

Directives can be changed:

**Revocation Methods:** Oral, written, or by destruction.

**Automatic Revocation:** Some states revoke upon divorce.

**Amendments:** Changes to existing directives.

**Effective Date:** When new directive takes effect.

Verification must reflect current status: "Superseded by directive dated [date]."

**Emergency Access**

Critical care access:

**24/7 Availability:** Verification must work around the clock.

**EMS Integration:** Paramedic access to verification systems.

**Wallet Cards:** Physical cards referencing verified directives.

**Medical Alert Bracelets:** Integration with medical alert systems.

**Capacity Questions**

When directive was executed:

**Presumption of Capacity:** Adults presumed competent.

**Capacity Assessment:** May require physician determination.

**Challenge Procedures:** How to challenge capacity.

**Timing:** Directive must be executed while capable.

Verification doesn't determine capacity but confirms directive was accepted.

**Multi-State Recognition**

Traveling patients:

**Full Faith and Credit:** General recognition of out-of-state directives.

**Substantial Compliance:** Most states accept substantially similar forms.

**Portability Challenges:** Practical difficulties with cross-state recognition.

**Electronic Registries:** Help bridge state boundaries.

Verification should indicate: "Executed in [state], verifiable in all states."

**EHR Integration:** Directives accessible in electronic records.

**HIE Networks:** Health information exchange sharing.

**Patient Portals:** Patient access to their directive status.

**Alert Systems:** Alerts when directive is on file.
