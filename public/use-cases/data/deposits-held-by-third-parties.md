# Deposits Held by Third Parties

You gave someone money to hold. You have a receipt. But is the money still there?

This summary covers the universal pattern: an individual or business pays a deposit to a third party, who is supposed to hold it safely and return it (or apply it) under specified conditions. The depositor wants to:

1. **Verify the deposit exists** in the holder's records
2. **Subscribe to status changes** (applied, released, forfeited, transferred)
3. **Prove the deposit** to others (lenders, courts, buyers)

Unlike NAV statements, verification here confirms **existence and status**, not the amount. The depositor already knows how much they paid — they want to know it's still there.

**The Concern:** Landlords are required (in most jurisdictions) to hold security deposits in segregated escrow accounts, often interest-bearing. Tenants worry:
- Is the deposit actually in escrow, or did the landlord spend it?
- When I move out, will they claim I never paid?
- Is the escrow account real, or a fabricated receipt?

**Verification Status:**
- **Held** — Deposit in escrow, no claims against it
- **Partial Claim** — Landlord has made a claim (damage, unpaid rent) — amount pending
- **Released** — Returned to tenant
- **Forfeited** — Applied to damages/unpaid rent (dispute window may apply)
- **Transferred** — Property sold; new owner assumed deposit obligation

**Third-Party Use:**
- **New landlords** (during property sale) — Verify deposit obligations they're assuming
- **Tenant attorneys** — Prove deposit existed when landlord claims otherwise
- **Housing courts** — Adjudicate deposit disputes

**The Concern:** Equity and semi-equity clubs collect large initiation deposits, refundable (partially) upon resignation. Members worry:
- Is the refund queue real, or will I wait forever?
- If the club goes bankrupt, is my deposit protected?
- Can the club change refund terms after I join?

**Verification Status:**
- **On Deposit** — Member in good standing
- **Resignation Filed** — Member on refund waitlist
- **Refund Eligible** — Reached front of queue
- **Refunded** — Payment issued
- **Forfeited** — Member expelled for cause (rare)

## Privacy: What Verification Returns

Verification confirms **existence and status**, not amounts:

| Verification Returns | Verification Does NOT Return |
|---------------------|------------------------------|
| "Held" / "Released" / "Forfeited" | Dollar amount |
| Status date | Depositor's identity to third parties |
| Next expected status change | Full account history |
| Holder confirmation | Other depositors' information |

**Why:** The depositor already knows how much they paid. Verification proves the holder acknowledges the deposit exists and its current status. Returning amounts would create privacy risks and potential targeting.

---

## Subscription to Changes

Unlike one-time document verification, deposit holders should offer **status subscriptions**:

- Email/webhook notification when status changes
- Advance notice before release or forfeiture
- Annual confirmation that deposit is still held

This addresses the core anxiety: "I gave them money years ago. Is it still there?" Rather than requiring the depositor to periodically verify, the system pushes status changes — but verification confirms subscription is active.

**Verification can confirm:**
- Depositor is subscribed to status changes
- Last notification date
- Next scheduled confirmation


## Jurisdictional Witnessing

A jurisdiction may require the issuer to retain a **witnessing firm** for regulatory compliance. The witnessing firm:

- Receives all hashes from the issuer, and any subsequent changes to the payload as they happen—which may manifest as a new hash, a status change, or even a 404 (record deleted)
- Receives structured content/metadata (key identifiers and dates)
- Does **NOT** receive plaintext or sensitive personal information
- Provides an immutable, timestamped audit trail—available to the jurisdiction on demand, to document holders/third parties during disputes, or as expert witness testimony in legal proceedings

This provides:
- **Non-repudiation:** Issuer cannot deny issuing the document
- **Timestamp proof:** Document existed at a specific time
- **Regulatory audit:** Jurisdictions can inspect the witness ledger for fraud detection
- **Resilience:** Verification works even if issuer's systems go down

**Public Blockchain (Non-Party)**

Witnessing firms may periodically commit rollups to an inexpensive public blockchain, providing an ultimate immutability guarantee. The blockchain is a "non-party"—infrastructure, not a participant in the transaction. This creates multiple verification paths:

1. **Issuer domain** — Direct check against the issuer
2. **Witnessing firm** — Independent confirmation with timestamp
3. **Public blockchain** — Decentralized trust anchor via rollup inclusion
